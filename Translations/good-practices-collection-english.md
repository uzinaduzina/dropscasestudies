This document, titled "**DSD_Best Practices**" presents one of the key outcomes of the Erasmus+ project “***Drops of Sustainable Development: Sustainable Development Competencies through Innovative Methodologies***”.

The project, led by Asociatia Uzinaduzina (RO) and carried out in collaboration with Petit Pas aps (IT), Asociatia Centrul European pentru Integrare Socioprofesionala (RO), Cross Culture International Foundation Cyprus (CY), SOLUCIONES TECNO-PROFESIONALES CONSULTING (ES), and Udruga 4 elementa (HR), aims to provide adult educators with innovative tools and strategies to integrate sustainable development competencies into their training programs.

To achieve this goal, the project explores the use of the TEAL (Technology Enhanced Active Learning) methodology, promoting active and collaborative learning through technology. The initiative stems from the need to support educators and trainers in overcoming the challenges of integrating sustainability into curricula by offering practical and engaging methods. Through the application of the TEAL method, the project not only provides innovative educational tools but also creates dynamic learning environments that facilitate active participation and the development of concrete solutions to sustainability-related issues.

Key Project Outcomes:

- **DSD_Best Practices**: A collection of best practices for sustainability education through TEAL.

- **DSD_Toolkit**: Educational modules for educators, featuring practical and participatory methods based on TEAL.

- **DSD_E-learning Platform**: A digital platform providing access to the developed resources.

- **DSD_Interactive Simulation Exercises**: Interactive exercises for adult learners aimed at developing skills for ecological transition.

The main project outcomes were achieved through the application of innovative and practical methods, as described in the educational modules and developed platforms. However, for these resources to have a concrete impact, it was essential to select best practices that were effective, replicable, and sustainable. The best practices collected and presented in this document were selected through a process that combined academic research with the analysis of educational and community projects focused on sustainable development education and the use of technological tools in education. This approach helped identify the most promising practices, ensuring that they met the needs of educators and communities while contributing to the successful integration of sustainability competencies into educational programs.

Selection Criteria:

- **Impact:** Significant contribution to increasing awareness and competencies in sustainability.

- **Innovation:** Original and creative approaches in the educational context.

- **Replicability:** Ease of adaptation to different educational settings.

- **Participation:** Active involvement of students, educators, and communities.

- **Sustainability:** Ability to maintain positive effects in the long term.

The application of these criteria allowed for the identification of practices that can be effectively adopted in different educational contexts, facilitating their dissemination and implementation.

In a global context that demands urgent action to address environmental and social challenges, adult education plays a crucial role in promoting sustainability. This document presents innovative methods and approaches aimed at fostering conscious and active citizens in sustainability promotion through the TEAL methodology.

To better understand these approaches and find inspiration for your own educational practice, we encourage you to continue reading.

<th colspan="2"><strong>1.2 Description:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">In 2022, UNESCO conducted a global survey revealing that 70% of young people couldn't explain climate change, prompting the launch of the Greening Education Partnership at the UN Transforming Education Summit. The Green School Quality Standard (UNESCO, 2024) provides a unified framework to help schools become sustainable and climate resilient by defining what it means to be “climate ready.” It promotes a whole institution approach structured around four key dimensions: school governance, facilities and operations, teaching and learning, and community engagement to integrate sustainability into all aspects of education. The standard’s objectives include promoting sustainability and climate readiness, offering a common framework for accreditation, encouraging continuous improvement, and aligning educational efforts with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Its target audience includes accreditation bodies, governments, schools, and broader educational stakeholders such as teachers, students, and communities, all working together to green at least 50% of schools worldwide by 2030.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.3 Context:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">The practice was implemented in response to global climate challenges and educational gaps in climate change awareness. It includes formal settings (e.g., primary, secondary, higher education) to informal settings(e.g., technical and vocational education and training TVET and community learning centers) Designed for a wide range of institutions from early childhood to higher education, including TVET and community learning centers, it ensures sustainability education spans the full learning journey. The standard has a global scope, with pilot implementation in diverse countries across Africa, Asia, Latin America, and the MENA region, reflecting varied cultural, socio-economic and environmental contexts. Rooted in youth demand, it promotes inclusive, climate resilient education for learners of all backgrounds.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.4 Selection Criteria:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Impact:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Improvements in awareness and skills related to sustainability included greater understanding among educators and students about environmental issues, the integration of sustainability into curricula, enhanced capacity to address local environmental challenges, and the development of practical green skills, all of which contributed to building climate-resilient schools and communities.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Innovation:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">The <em>Green School Quality Standard</em> introduces several innovative approaches to make schools climate ready and sustainable. It adopts a <strong>Whole-Institution Approach</strong> (WIA), engaging all aspects of school life governance, operations, curriculum, and community. Its <strong>flexible, resource-based action planning</strong> let's schools adapt activities to their capacities. <strong>Student empowerment</strong> is central, with learners involved in Green Committees, campaigns, and decision-making. It values <strong>indigenous and local knowledge</strong>, promotes <strong>hands-on learning</strong> through green infrastructure and environmental monitoring, and aligns with global frameworks like the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and the Comprehensive School Safety Framework (CSSF), bridging safety, resilience, and sustainability in education. Importantly, it embraces a <strong>practice-informed, non-prescriptive model</strong>, making it adaptable and inclusive across contexts.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Replicability:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">This practice offers a flexible, inclusive approach to integrating sustainability into adult education by promoting whole institution practices in governance, operations, teaching, and community engagement. Adaptable to vocational training, community centers, and continuing education, the framework equips adult learners with practical green skills such as climate-smart agriculture, renewable energy, and sustainable entrepreneurship through experiential, culturally relevant learning. It encourages active learner participation in decision-making and community sustainability projects, while allowing institutions to start with low-cost initiatives and grow capacity over time. By prioritizing inclusivity and lifelong learning, the standard empowers adult learners as agents of green transitions and sustainable development.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Participation:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;"><p>UNESCO’s <em>Green School Quality Standard</em> actively engages students, teachers, and community members across governance, operations, teaching, and outreach to build <strong>green skills for sustainable work</strong>. By forming inclusive committees, involving <strong>students</strong> in eco-audits and green entrepreneurship, and embedding sustainability in hands-on curricula from composting to climate-smart projects learners develop practical competencies like problem-solving, collaboration, and environmental stewardship.</p>
<p><strong>Teachers</strong> serve as facilitators, guiding experiential learning and aligning sustainability with academic goals, while <strong>community members</strong> contribute to local knowledge and real-world relevance. Through joint service learning projects, capacity building workshops, and partnerships with businesses or civic groups, schools become hubs of climate action and workforce readiness. This collaborative model cultivates leadership, technical know-how, and a strong sustainability ethic equipping all stakeholders, especially adult and youth learners, with the skills and mindset needed for a green, resilient future.</p></th>
</tr>

<th colspan="2" style="text-align: center;"><p><em>„Warka Tower is designed to harvest water from the atmosphere (rain, fog, dew) providing an alternative water source for rural populations that face challenges accessing drinkable water.“</em></p>
<p>Designed by Italian architect Arturo Vittori, the Warka Tower represents an innovative solution for collecting potable water in rural areas with scarce water resources. Inspired by traditional water harvesting techniques and the principles of biomimicry (the imitation of natural processes), the tower harnesses the properties of natural and local materials, such as bamboo and plant fibers, to extract water from the atmosphere.</p>
<p>In summary, the tower's lattice-like structure, made of bamboo, supports an internal mesh of natural fibers. This mesh captures moisture present in the air in the form of fog, dew, and water vapor, and the water droplets that form on the mesh through condensation slide downwards, where they are collected in a tank at the base of the tower. The use of natural and local materials makes the Warka Tower environmentally friendly and low-impact. Furthermore, its modular structure facilitates assembly, maintenance, and adaptation to various environmental conditions.</p>
<p>The Warka Tower simultaneously provides an alternative and sustainable water source, reducing dependence on traditional water sources that are often scarce or contaminated, a meeting point for local communities, promoting knowledge sharing and raising awareness about the importance of sustainable water resource management, and the ease of construction and maintenance of the tower allows communities to autonomously manage their water resource, fostering local development and resilience.</p>
<p>In conclusion, the Warka Tower is an example of how innovation and sustainability can combine to address global challenges related to water scarcity, offering concrete and low-impact solutions for the most vulnerable communities.</p></th>
</tr>

</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.1.1 Picture if available</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: center;"><img src="good-practices-collection-english-media/english-image-14.jpg" style="width:6.64531in;height:1.88976in" alt="Slider-site-2025-1024x300-1-0x0.jpeg" /></th>

<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.2 Description:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">Patrula de Reciclare (Recycling Patrol) is a nationwide environmental education program launched in 2011 to engage students and teachers in responsible e-waste recycling. Its objectives are to raise awareness of sustainable waste management and to actively involve youth in collecting and recycling electronic waste. School-based teams (“patrols”) of students, guided by coordinating teachers, organize e-waste collection campaigns, educational workshops, and community outreach. The target audience is primarily K-12 students and educators across Romania, with broader impact on local communities through awareness campaigns. Over the years, the program has grown into a vibrant network of schools and volunteers, fostering environmental responsibility and hands-on learning for sustainability.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.3 Context:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">This good practice is implemented in the formal education context as an extracurricular nationwide initiative. Schools from all types (primary, secondary, urban, rural, including special education institutions) voluntarily join the program each year. Romania’s need for improved electronic waste recycling (being decades behind EU standards in 2011) provided the backdrop. The program is coordinated by environmental NGOs (initially Asociația RoRec, now Asociația Volens) in partnership with national authorities. By 2021, Patrula de Reciclare had active participation in hundreds of schools across all counties, including small rural schools and large urban schools, making sustainable development education accessible in diverse geographical and demographic settings. The initiative often runs in parallel with the school year, with dedicated campaigns (e.g., Earth Hour, EU Green Week) that integrate into the school’s activities.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.4 Selection Criteria:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Impact:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">The program has demonstrated significant improvements in sustainability awareness and action among participants. Patrula de Reciclare has involved over 4,300 teachers and 118,000 children and youths nationwide, who together collected more than 8 million kilograms of waste electrical and electronic equipment for recycling. This collective effort not only diverted substantial e-waste from landfills, but also cultivated eco-conscious habits in hundreds of local communities. Participants report greater knowledge of recycling and a sense of empowerment in contributing to environmental protection. Notably, the initiative provided a successful inclusion experience for students with disabilities or those at risk of school abandonment, by engaging them in collaborative environmental actions. Such outcomes indicate a broad improvement in sustainability-related skills (project management, teamwork, environmental stewardship) and awareness across diverse student groups.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Innovation:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Patrula de Reciclare introduced a creative, hands-on approach to ESD in Romania at a time when waste education was minimal. It employs an original model of “patrol” teams that turn recycling into an interactive, goal-oriented challenge for schools. This approach was novel in leveraging student enthusiasm and friendly competition to drive community recycling efforts. The program regularly integrates creative activities – from art made of recycled materials to digital storytelling about the environment – and has expanded to cover contemporary themes like climate change and circular economy. Its innovative public outreach (such as national contests and eco-pledge campaigns) and the High Patronage it received in 2015 underscored its unique role in Romania’s educational landscape. Patrula’s success and originality have been recognized internationally: it was invited by the European Commission as a benchmark project in the launch of the European Education for Environment Coalition, highlighting its pioneering status in climate education programs.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Replicability:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">This practice is highly adaptable to other educational contexts. The “Recycling Patrol” model can be easily replicated by any school or community group with basic guidance on organizing collection campaigns and partnerships with recycling authorities. In fact, the program was scaled up from an initial pilot to a platform of excellence for environmental education, with the intention that its methods be adopted broadly. The structure – forming student eco-teams, setting local recycling targets, and celebrating achievements – is simple and flexible enough to apply in different regions or countries. Many schools sustain the practice year after year, and new schools join annually, evidencing its adaptability. The concept has inspired similar youth-led environmental initiatives beyond e-waste (e.g., battery recycling drives, tree planting patrols), showing that the core approach can be transferred to various sustainability topics.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Participation:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Active involvement of students, teachers, and the community is at the heart of Patrula de Reciclare. Students take on key roles as “eco-ambassadors,” designing posters, giving presentations, and leading collection events in their neighborhoods. Teachers mentor the students and often integrate the project with classroom lessons (science, civic education, etc.), reinforcing learning through practice. Parents and local community members are engaged by contributing their used electronics to the school’s recycling drives. To date, tens of thousands of citizens have been indirectly reached and mobilized by these student-led actions. The program’s collaborative nature also extends to partnerships: schools work with local recycling companies and authorities who help remove the collected e-waste. This multi-level participation – from individual students to national agencies – not only enriches the learning experience but also builds a community ethos around sustainable practices. Moreover, the inclusivity of the program (involving students of different ages, abilities, and backgrounds) has strengthened social cohesion; participants learn from each other and feel collectively responsible for the environmental outcome.</th>
</tr>

Quantitatively, as noted, it removed over 8 million kiolograms of e-waste from the environment, contributing directly to Romania’s recycling targets. It built a nationwide network of environmentally conscious educators and students; by its 10th year, 4,300 teachers and 118,000 students had participated, many schools establishing permanent recycling points as a result of the project. Qualitatively, participating schools report heightened environmental awareness and activism among their students. <th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;"><span id="_up0yv0yn41jy" class="anchor"></span>Eco-Școala (Eco-Schools Program) – EDUCATION FOR SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT IN SCHOOLS</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.1.1 Picture if available</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: center;"><img src="good-practices-collection-english-media/english-image-20.png" style="width:4.91728in;height:2.28346in" alt="Screenshot 2025-04-01 at 13.09.32.png" /></th>

<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.2 Description:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">The Eco-Școala program is the Romanian implementation of the international Eco-Schools initiative, which integrates Education for Sustainable Development into the daily life of schools. Coordinated nationally by the Carpatho-Danubian Center for Geoecology (CCDG) since 1999, this program guides schools through a structured framework to improve their environmental performance and sustainability education. Its objectives include incorporating environmental topics (such as waste, water, energy, biodiversity, etc.) into the curriculum and school culture, reducing the school’s ecological footprint, and empowering students to take leadership in “greening” their school. The target audience spans all pre-university education levels – from kindergartens to high schools – that voluntarily enroll in the program. Schools that successfully implement the required steps (forming an Eco-Committee, auditing their environmental impact, creating an action plan, and monitoring progress) over a two-year cycle are awarded the Green Flag certification, an internationally recognized symbol of excellence in ESD. Through Eco-Școala, students engage in projects like school gardens, recycling drives, energy saving campaigns, and community clean-ups, making sustainability a tangible and continuous learning experience.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.3 Context:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">Eco-Școala was introduced in Romania at a time when environmental education was largely extracurricular. Since its debut with 5 pilot schools in 1999, it has expanded dramatically within the Romanian education system. The program operates in public and private schools across all regions of Romania, under various socio-economic conditions. By the end of the 2023/2024 school year, approximately 850 school units nationwide (including primary schools, secondary schools, high schools, vocational schools, special education institutions, and even children’s clubs) have participated in Eco-Școala at some point, with 265 schools currently active in the program. This widespread adoption illustrates the program’s adaptability to local contexts – from rural village schools to large urban colleges. The national coordination by CCDG provides training sessions, educational resources, and an annual seminar to share best practices. Local authorities often support schools in their sustainability projects (for example, city halls may help with tree-planting events or waste management facilities). Given Romania’s commitment to the UN Sustainable Development Goals and the Ministry of Education’s encouragement of environmental initiatives, Eco-Școala has become a cornerstone of ESD in the country, fostering a culture of sustainability in formal education.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>1.4 Selection Criteria:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Impact:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">The Eco-Școala program has a broad and sustained impact on raising sustainability awareness and skills among students and educators. Thousands of Romanian students each year learn to assess and improve their immediate environment through this program. Since its inception, around 850 educational institutions in Romania have implemented Eco-Școala’s 7-step methodology, and as of 2024, 265 schools actively hold the Green Flag status, reflecting ongoing commitment. The impact is evident in tangible improvements: participating schools report reduced waste and energy use on campus, creation of green spaces (like small gardens or eco-corners in classrooms), and improved recycling habits in the school community. Moreover, students involved in Eco-Școala demonstrate greater knowledge of environmental issues and more responsible attitudes. They learn to link local environmental problems to global sustainability challenges, and they gain practical skills in project management and eco-innovation. Teachers also benefit by deepening their understanding of sustainable development and learning new pedagogical approaches for ESD. The program’s influence often extends beyond the school walls – many Eco-Școala institutions become local hubs for environmental action, organizing community events such as Earth Day fairs, environmental contests, or neighborhood clean-ups. Through qualitative outcomes like increased student leadership and quantitative outcomes like waste reductions, Eco-Școala significantly advances ESD goals in Romanian education.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Innovation:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Eco-Școala introduced a pioneering, student-centered approach to environmental education in Romania. Its innovation lies in making students key actors in driving change: each school forms an Eco Committee that typically includes students, teachers, parents, and local stakeholders, giving students a voice in decision-making about the school’s sustainability practices. This was a novel concept for Romanian schools, creating a participatory governance model at the school level. The program is holistic, covering up to 12 thematic areas of sustainability (from climate change and biodiversity to waste and healthy living) , and encourages creative, project-based learning in each area. Students might conduct experiments on water quality, develop awareness campaigns, or design innovative solutions like solar chargers or rainwater harvesting systems, depending on the themes chosen. The continuous improvement cycle (plan-do-check-act) built into Eco-Școala ensures that innovation is ongoing; schools often set new targets every year, fostering a culture of continuous creative problem-solving. At an international level, Eco-Schools is supported by the EU, UNESCO, and UNEP and is implemented in 68 countries worldwide. Romanian schools, by participating, join this international network – an innovative aspect that enables cross-cultural exchange of ideas (some Romanian Eco-Schools have twinned with schools abroad to share project innovations). Thus, Eco-Școala’s framework itself is an innovative educational model, and it continually spurs schools to devise original, student-led sustainability initiatives.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Replicability:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Replicability is a core strength of Eco-Școala – the program is explicitly designed to be replicated across different contexts and has been scaled globally. In Romania, it has been successfully adapted to all types of schools (small rural schools, large urban schools, kindergartens, technical colleges, etc.), demonstrating flexibility. The requirements for participation (such as forming a committee, doing an environmental review, and implementing at least two sustainability themes) are clear and attainable, yet allow each school to tailor activities to its reality. For instance, one school might focus on waste and water conservation, while another emphasizes biodiversity and energy, based on local needs. The availability of a detailed guide and support from CCDG makes it easy for new schools to adopt the program’s methodology. Many elements of Eco-Școala can also be replicated informally: even schools that are not officially in the program have used the 7-step framework as a model to start their own green initiatives. The fact that 265+ Romanian schools remain active indicates that the practice is sustainable and can be re-run annually with new student cohorts. Additionally, the Eco-Școala concept has been replicated through national campaigns like “Școala Verde” (Green School) and “Săptămâna Verde” (Green Week) introduced by the Ministry of Education, which borrow from the Eco-Schools philosophy to spread ESD practices nationwide. Overall, the program’s structure and success stories provide a replicable blueprint for any educational institution aiming to integrate sustainable development into its teaching and operations.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Participation:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Active participation is fundamentally built into Eco-Școala’s design. The program requires whole-school involvement: active support from the school leadership and local authorities, genuine decision-making roles for students, and engagement of the entire teaching staff. Students participate through the Eco Committee and various working teams; they investigate issues (like auditing how much energy the school uses), propose solutions, and implement action plans (such as starting a recycling program or creating a butterfly garden). This gives students a sense of ownership and responsibility for their school environment – they become “protagonists of their own education,” developing leadership and collaboration skills in the process. Teachers participate by embedding ESD topics into lessons and by mentoring student projects, often working across disciplines (e.g. a biology and art teacher might co-lead a project on creating recycled art to depict ecosystems). School principals and administrators are involved by aligning school policies (like procurement or maintenance) with the green action plans students create, demonstrating institutional participation. Parents and community members are also drawn in – many Eco-Schools host community events (tree plantings, eco-fairs) and collaborate with local businesses or NGOs for resources and expertise. This broad participation ensures that the program is not an isolated effort but a communal one, reinforcing the values of sustainability at multiple levels. The participatory approach has proven effective in changing mindsets: students influence their families’ habits (such as energy saving at home), and teachers often become sustainability champions in their broader community. In summary, Eco-Școala actively involves all stakeholders in the education process, creating a participatory culture that extends from the classroom to the community.</th>
</tr>

<th colspan="2"><strong>1.6 Results:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">In over two decades of implementation in Romania, the Eco-Școala program has yielded substantial results in cultivating sustainable schools. Quantitative results include the large number of schools engaged (265 currently active, cumulatively 850+ involved) and the repeated renewal of Green Flags – many schools have maintained their Eco-Școala status for multiple cycles, with some proudly renewing the Green Flag 5 or more times, indicating long-term commitment . Schools have implemented countless practical improvements: for example, schools have reported cutting down their waste by significant percentages after starting recycling and composting, or reducing electricity consumption by switching to energy-efficient lighting as part of student-driven action plans. Some schools have secured small grants or community funding to install solar panels, rainwater harvesting systems, or to plant trees, directly improving local environmental quality. Qualitative results are evident in the transformative educational experiences seen at Eco-Schools. Students involved often show increased environmental literacy and civic engagement; they are more likely to volunteer and initiate sustainability projects outside school. Teachers note that project-based involvement in Eco-Școala boosts student motivation and teamwork skills. The ethos of “learning by doing” has led to innovative student projects—ranging from designing eco-fashion shows with reused materials to conducting awareness campaigns on biodiversity in their towns—that have garnered local and even national recognition. The program’s community impact is also significant: many Eco-Școala activities involve local residents (e.g., neighborhood cleanup events), thereby raising environmental awareness beyond the school. Importantly, Eco-Școala has institutionalized ESD in many participating schools; environmental topics have been integrated into school curricula and annual plans as a direct result of the program. Schools often share their successes at the annual national Eco-Școala symposium, creating a ripple effect of inspiration. In summary, the Eco-Școala good practice has led to greener school operations, empowered students as change agents, and forged stronger school-community partnerships for sustainable development . Its longevity and continual growth in Romania attest to its effectiveness in nurturing a generation of environmentally conscious citizens.</th>

<th colspan="2"><strong>2.2 Description:</strong></th>

<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;"><p>Flipped Learning is a pedagogical approach that reverses the traditional learning model. Students access educational content, such as video lessons or readings, at home before the classroom lesson. This allows classroom time to be dedicated to interactive activities, such as discussions, problem-solving, and group work, with the support of the teacher, fostering greater interaction between students and the teacher. Flipped Learning is predominantly used in adult education and focuses on active learning, student autonomy, and the personalization of the learning experience. Here are the main characteristics:</p>
<ul>
<li><p><u>Reversal of the traditional model</u>: Flipped Learning (or "flipped classroom") reverses the traditional teaching model. Instead of receiving lessons in the classroom and doing homework at home, students study theoretical content at home, through videos, readings, or other resources, and use classroom time for practical and interactive activities.</p></li>
<li><p><u>Active learning</u>: The main goal is to transform the classroom into an active learning environment, where students can apply acquired knowledge, solve problems, discuss, and collaborate with their peers and the teacher.</p></li>
<li><p><u>Autonomy and personalization</u>: This approach is particularly well-suited to adult education, as it promotes student autonomy and allows for the personalization of the learning experience. Adults can study theoretical materials at their own pace and according to their needs.</p></li>
<li><p><u>Student-centered learning</u>: Flipped Learning places the student at the center, making them the protagonist of their own learning journey. The teacher assumes the role of facilitator, guiding students and supporting them in practical activities.</p></li>
<li><p><u>Flexibility</u>: Adult education often has to deal with limited schedules and availability. Flipped learning, due to its nature, offers greater flexibility.</p></li>
<li><p><u>Digital skills</u>: The use of video lessons and online materials implies an improvement in digital skills, a quality increasingly required in the world of work.</p></li>
</ul></th>
</tr>
<tr>

<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;"><p>In this way, future teachers learn to work with autonomy and personal responsibility, but also with positive interdependence, communicating effectively with their classmates. The fact that it is through technological means promotes greater interaction by optimizing learning outcomes.</p>
<p>Among the main objectives we highlight:</p>
<ol type="1">
<li><p>Collaborative learning through Padlet</p></li>
<li><p>To enhance digital competences through critical reflection of students</p></li>
<li><p>Involve students in the approach to responsible use of social networks from a preventive perspective and improve emotional management, addressing the challenges posed by research on these topics (Olweus &amp; Limber, 2018)</p></li>

<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;"><p>From the approach of the Edelvives Foundation Chair of Comprehensive Educational Care of the University of Zaragoza, one of the aspects of this care encompasses the improvement of interpersonal relationships at a virtual level, facing this challenge through cybercoexistence and the promotion of collaborative online learning.</p>
<p>We would like to point out that it has been implemented in subjects of the Degree in Teaching in Primary Education of the Faculty of Education of the University of Zaragoza during the 2019-2020 academic year and has positive implications that can be transferred to others.</p></th>

<tr>
<th style="text-align: left;"><strong>Effectiveness:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;"><p>The students who received the experience were made up of 153 students.</p>
<p>Both collaborative work, the implementation of information and communication technologies, at the service of improving teacher training to address cybercoexistence, are fundamental.</p>
<p>Each group of 3-5 students defined a topic to develop around cybercoexistence: netiquette, cyberbullying, digital identity, digital footprint, fake news, privacy, etc. Both the process and the final product of the work (an educational video) were asked to work through their own Padlet wall. Throughout the semester, each group designed and shared through this online tool different documents that marked the process of making the final video, such as, for example, the rundown of the video they were designing or the symbaloo generated with materials sought by the members of the group on the same topic as a repository of inspiration for their own design. Each group shared the wall with the teachers.</p>
<p>We have been able to verify that the perception of the students is that motivation for the subject, responsibility and that they have learned to collaborate through a digital application. In this sense, we also note their satisfaction with the use of the Padlet tool. These results are in line with previous work that includes other educational experiences at the university</p>
<p>The assessment of the success of the online tools used has been obtained through the weekly evaluation with the students on them, so that we included the necessary improvements constantly throughout the process.</p></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th style="text-align: left;"><strong>Technology:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;"><p>This didactic experience has had the Padlet tool as its backbone. This online platform aims to provide an interactive mural. In addition, the platform allows the user to share multimedia resources from different sources (websites, videos, images, etc.). Some of its advantages are that it is very intuitive, can be used with any device and allows simultaneous collaboration once the user has registered in the application. In addition, it is possible to download the files that are shared on the wall. Padlet applications for the academic environment can be very diverse. For example, it can be used to collect ideas on a topic, evaluate and co-evaluate a piece of work, as well as run a project collaboratively as is the case. It is a tool that has a free version and a paid version with a greater possibility of functions and number of walls to use.</p>
<p>It encompasses a collaborative online and interdisciplinary perspective of the subject, with a relationship of several topics within the program and with a Service Learning approach so that it is useful to different groups of teachers and students in educational centers.</p>
<p>The aim is to promote innovation and the initiation of research, promoting the transfer, from a feedback of the collaborative work of teachers of the CEIP Ramiro Solans, students, management team with the students of the Degree in Primary Education of the Faculty of Education.</p></th>

The project consists of creating educational material in video format for 5th and 6th year of Primary Education on the responsible use of the Internet and cybercoexistence. The guidelines for carrying out the project are presented to them through a wall of the Padlet application, which served to explain the online collaborative tool.</p>
<p>The comprehensive training of future teachers is improved, which provides the design of materials for their subsequent use from a Service Learning approach. They are able to exhibit in public, in front of a classroom in an educational center, part of the work they have done. A high degree of motivation is evident, since they give greater meaning and usefulness to this type of work carried out in the Faculty. They approach a real environment and lay the foundations, at the level of concerns, doubts and challenges, for future subjects that are in line with deepening digital skills.</p></th>

<p>The work is linked to the Innovation Project of the University of Zaragoza, in its implementation. This Project has been developed, with different changes over the years and the participants find it relevant to highlight that to the extent that students are involved in different actions with educational centers, to this extent we consider that it has the possibility of being maintained and consolidated over time.</p></th>

<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;"><span id="_egwdmw34dym2" class="anchor"></span>Digitaliada – Digital Education for Active Learning in Rural Schools</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>2.1.1 Picture if available</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: center;"><img src="good-practices-collection-english-media/english-image-03.png" style="width:4.72441in;height:4.72441in" alt="Digitaliada_2024_proiect_Fundatia_Orange.png" /></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>2.2 Description:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">Digitaliada is a national program that leverages technology to enhance active learning, particularly in Romanian rural schools. Initiated in 2016 by the Orange Foundation, Digitaliada provides a comprehensive model for integrating digital tools into the teaching and learning process. The program’s objectives are to improve students’ engagement and understanding of subjects (especially math and ICT) through interactive digital content, and to build teachers’ capacity to use technology in student-centered ways. Digitaliada equips participating schools with modern educational technology (tablets for students, laptops for teachers, projectors, interactive whiteboards, etc.) and offers an extensive repository of curriculum-aligned digital learning materials. It also includes a strong teacher training component: educators receive accredited courses and ongoing mentoring on how to conduct active learning lessons using apps, educational games, and online platforms. The target audience is middle school students (grades 5–8, roughly ages 11–14) and their teachers in underserved areas, though the program has expanded to some urban schools as well. In a typical Digitaliada classroom, students might learn mathematics by playing an interactive game or solve problems collaboratively on tablets, while the teacher facilitates and offers immediate feedback via an e-learning platform. By combining technology with active pedagogies (like project-based learning and gamification), Digitaliada aims to make learning more attractive and effective for students.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>2.3 Context:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">Digitaliada was conceived in response to the educational disparities between urban and rural areas in Romania, particularly in terms of access to digital resources and modern teaching methods. Many rural schools struggled with outdated materials and limited internet access. The program began by selecting a cohort of pilot rural schools and gradually scaling up each year. As of the 2022-2023 school year, Digitaliada had been implemented in 101 schools across 34 counties, directly reaching over 30,000 students and training more than 3,000 teachers in digital pedagogy . Most of these schools are small and located in villages or small towns, where Digitaliada often provided the first fully equipped digital lab. The socio-economic context of these communities made the program particularly impactful: for many students, it was the first time they used a tablet or experienced internet-based learning. The Digitaliada program is aligned with Romania’s broader push for digital education (such as the Smart-Edu strategy 2021–2027), and it has been recognized by the Ministry of Education – the teacher training courses in the program are accredited, and the Ministry has collaborated in disseminating the open educational resources created. During the COVID-19 pandemic, Digitaliada’s existing e-learning platform and content became invaluable, enabling continuous education online for the schools involved and even for others who accessed its open resources. The program’s context is therefore one of addressing digital divide challenges and modernizing teaching in the formal education system, with strong support from a private foundation in partnership with educational authorities.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>2.4 Selection Criteria:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Effectiveness:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Digitaliada has shown clear evidence of increasing student interaction and understanding. Classrooms in the program report a shift from passive learning to highly interactive sessions – students regularly work in teams on tablets, participate in educational games and quizzes, and receive instant feedback on their work, which keeps them engaged. According to program data, the introduction of digital interactive content has improved students’ performance in subjects like mathematics; teachers noted higher retention of concepts when students practiced via apps (e.g., using GeoGebra for visualizing geometry) . The Digitaliada platform hosts thousands of exercises and automatic quizzes, which have been successfully used in over 100 schools around the country to enhance lessons and homework . This wide usage indicates the material’s effectiveness and adaptability. Teachers also observe that students who were previously shy or less involved became more active when learning with technology, as the variety of media (videos, interactive simulations, etc.) caters to different learning styles. In terms of measurable outcomes, some Digitaliada schools have seen improved exam readiness; for instance, the program developed a dedicated series of 10 video lessons and online tests for the national Grade 8 exam, which provided personalized feedback to students and helped boost their confidence and results . Overall, Digitaliada’s approach—blending technology with active learning—has effectively increased student participation in class and improved understanding of complex topics by allowing students to learn by doing and by exploring at their own pace.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Technology:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">The program makes innovative use of technological tools to support learning. Each Digitaliada school is provided with a kit that typically includes class sets of tablets (allowing each student to have hands-on access), laptops for teachers, video projectors or interactive flat panels, document cameras, and even graphics tablets for digital handwriting . In addition to hardware, Digitaliada offers a rich digital ecosystem: an online platform with over 4,000 open educational resources (interactive lessons, exercises, and tutorials) aligned to the national curriculum , and recommended educational software/apps for various subjects . What’s innovative is not just the presence of technology, but how it’s applied – teachers are trained to use these tools in active pedagogies (for example, using a quiz app for real-time class polls, or having students create their own digital stories). The program also integrates emerging technologies like AI-driven tools; a recent update introduced an AI-based feature on the platform to assist in lesson creation and homework help . This keeps the technology aspect cutting-edge. Additionally, Digitaliada encourages content creation: it runs an annual contest where teachers and even students (14+) create and upload their own interactive digital lessons . This has led to a growing repository of locally made tech-enabled lessons, showcasing innovative uses of technology from the grassroots. In summary, Digitaliada employs technology not as a gimmick, but as a well-rounded means to enrich the curriculum – from hardware deployment in classrooms to development of custom Romanian-language digital content – thereby exemplifying TEAL through comprehensive tech integration.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Collaboration:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Collaboration is a key facet of the Digitaliada methodology, both among students and among educators. In the classroom, many Digitaliada activities are designed for teamwork – students often work in pairs or groups on tablets to solve problems, play collaborative learning games, or complete project-based assignments. This fosters peer learning; for example, a group might collaborate on a Scratch coding project or jointly research a topic online to create a presentation, thereby practicing teamwork and communication while using technology. Teachers have reported that students develop better teamwork skills as they discuss strategies to win an educational game or troubleshoot a digital challenge together. Moreover, Digitaliada created a community of practice for teachers: over 3,000 teachers form the “Digitaliada family,” sharing experiences and tips on using digital tools . The program conducts regional and national workshops where teachers from different schools meet (sometimes virtually) to collaborate on improving digital lesson plans. The online platform also serves as a collaborative hub – educators contribute open resources and provide feedback to each other (resources are reviewed and improved collaboratively by the community). There have been instances of inter-school collaboration as well: some Digitaliada schools have partnered for joint activities, such as coding hackathons or virtual math competitions, connecting students from different areas. The collaborative spirit is further seen in how the program materials were used during school closures – teachers collaborated to support each other and even volunteer outside the program, sharing Digitaliada resources widely. By promoting teamwork in learning activities and establishing a supportive teacher network, Digitaliada strongly embodies the collaboration criterion of TEAL.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Feedback:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">Immediate and constructive feedback mechanisms are built into the Digitaliada learning process. The program’s digital platform features automated quizzes and exercises where students get instant results and explanations for their answers. For example, the math and Romanian language test modules for exam preparation provide personalized feedback – when a student answers a question, the system immediately indicates if it’s correct and offers an explanation or hint for incorrect answers. This allows students to learn from mistakes in real-time and adjust their understanding on the spot. In the classroom setting, teachers use educational apps that have live response systems (like Kahoot! or Socrative) to gauge student understanding; students receive instant feedback on their devices as they participate in these quizzes, which also adds a level of gamified reinforcement. Digitaliada also encourages a culture of feedback in teaching practices: through the teacher training, educators learn to utilize digital assessment tools that track student progress, enabling them to give data-driven, timely feedback. For instance, a teacher can see analytics from the platform on which exercises students struggled with and then address those areas individually with those students. The digital content often includes interactive hints and scaffolded tasks – effectively guiding students step-by-step (a form of built-in feedback that directs learners). Outside of automated means, the technology frees up teachers’ time from administrative tasks, allowing them to circulate in the class and provide instant personal feedback as students work on tablets. In summary, Digitaliada’s implementation ensures students are not working in a vacuum with technology; rather, they receive continuous feedback through quizzes, interactive content, and teacher input, which reinforces learning and keeps students actively engaged in improving their performance.</th>

Quantitatively, the program’s scale is itself a result: as of 2023, over 30,000 students have benefitted from modern digital learning in their classrooms, and more than 3,000 teachers have been trained in active digital teaching techniques . The investment of over 3.2 million euros by the Orange Foundation has translated into tangible infrastructure in 100+ schools (for instance, 2,100 tablets and 300+ other devices were deployed ). Many of these schools, which previously had minimal ICT resources, now serve as local examples of digital transformation. In nationwide math assessments, some Digitaliada schools have reported improved average scores, attributing it to the engaging practice students get on the platform (though comprehensive data is still being gathered). <th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;"><span id="_83uc7xl2kjgz" class="anchor"></span>SmartLab Bucov – High-Tech Active Learning Laboratory in a Rural School</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>2.1.1 Picture if available</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: center;"><img src="good-practices-collection-english-media/english-image-02.jpg" style="width:6.29921in;height:4.72441in" alt="38BC959D-98DF-4712-AA27-941B236254C0.jpeg.jpeg" /></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>2.2 Description:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">SmartLab Bucov is an example of a Technology Enhanced Active Learning environment implemented in a Romanian rural school. Opened in 2021 at “Constantin Stere” Gymnasium School in Bucov (Prahova County), it is the first fully equipped SmartLab (intelligent digital laboratory) in a village school in Romania . This lab serves as a model for integrating cutting-edge technology – such as Virtual Reality (VR), 3D printing, and educational robotics – into the daily learning experience. The lab’s objective is to stimulate creativity, critical thinking, and collaboration among students by providing them with state-of-the-art tools and a flexible learning space. In SmartLab Bucov, traditional classroom desks have been replaced with collaborative workstations. Students can engage in active projects like designing objects with 3D modeling software and printing them on a 3D printer, programming multifunctional robots, or exploring virtual field trips with VR goggles. These activities align with and enhance the school curriculum: for instance, students learn geometry by creating shapes on the computer and printing them, or they study biology by virtually exploring ecosystems in VR. The target audience includes the school’s 500 students (aged roughly 6 to 15) and their teachers. The lab is used across subjects – from STEM classes to even literature (the space also includes a cozy reading corner to blend technology with reading) . SmartLab Bucov was realized through a public-private partnership (six private companies jointly funded the ~€120,000 cost) , highlighting a community investment in innovative education. In essence, this good practice transforms a rural classroom into a “future classroom,” demonstrating how active learning can be supercharged with technology.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>2.3 Context:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">The educational context of SmartLab Bucov is a public lower secondary school in a rural area, which traditionally had limited access to advanced technology. The initiative emerged as part of a broader vision (championed by organizations like INACO – Initiative for Competitiveness) to create smart labs in schools across Romania, ensuring that even rural students gain the digital skills needed for the future. The lab in Bucov is a pioneer: prior to this, such high-tech labs were typically found only in a few elite urban schools or universities. Its inauguration marked the first time rural students would have daily access to equipment like 3D scanners and VR sets. The lab is physically set up in a renovated space (the school’s attic), which was transformed with modern flooring, new furniture, and colorful décor to be inviting and conducive to creativity. This environment differs from standard classrooms: it’s open, with areas for hands-on experimentation and others for collaboration or relaxation (like the reading beanbag area), embodying a student-centric design. The social context is notable – Bucov’s SmartLab drew attention nationally (“even better than in Denmark!” a media headline quipped, underscoring its exceptional quality for a village school) and has since inspired additional labs. Following Bucov’s example, similar digital labs have been set up in other locations, including an urban SmartLab in Bucharest (School No. 311, Sector 6, in 2023) where an entire floor was redesigned to blend technology and learning in an appealing way. These contexts show that the SmartLab concept is spreading, supported by Romania’s National Recovery and Resilience Plan (PNRR) which allocated funds for equipping schools with such labs. In summary, SmartLab Bucov operates in a context of bridging educational inequity – bringing a top-tier active learning facility to a rural school – and it sits at the forefront of a nascent network of SmartLabs aiming to modernize education countrywide.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2"><strong>2.4 Selection Criteria:</strong></th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Effectiveness:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">The SmartLab has markedly increased student interaction and enthusiasm for learning. Since its opening, teachers report that the lab has become the “favorite place” of students at school. Instead of passively sitting through lectures, students in the SmartLab are constantly doing: they build, code, experiment, and explore. This high level of hands-on engagement has led to deeper understanding, especially in STEM subjects. For example, abstract concepts in physics or math become tangible when students can model them in 3D or simulate them in virtual reality. A teacher from Bucov noted that during robotics sessions, even students who typically struggle academically were fully absorbed in problem-solving to make their robot complete a task – a sign of improved focus and comprehension through interactive learning. The SmartLab’s effectiveness is also evident in skill acquisition: over the course of a year, many students became proficient in 3D design and basic programming, skills they did not have before. Formative feedback from the lab’s usage shows improved learning outcomes; teachers have cited increases in class participation and a willingness among students to tackle complex projects, thanks to the motivating environment. Furthermore, the SmartLab has drawn in students after hours – voluntary attendance in newly formed clubs (robotics club, 3D printing club) went up, indicating that learning feels less like an obligation and more like a passion in this setting. While quantitative test score data is still being collected, early indicators like increased science fair participation and better problem-solving scores in informatics contests suggest that the active learning enabled by SmartLab Bucov translates to academic and practical success.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Technology:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">SmartLab Bucov is equipped with an impressive array of cutting-edge technological tools applied in innovative ways for education. The lab includes multiple 3D printers (both monochrome and multicolor) and 3D scanners, which allow students to turn digital designs into physical objects and to scan real objects into digital models. It features about 15 VR headsets, enabling immersive virtual reality experiences – for instance, virtual science experiments or virtual field trips to historical sites. The lab also contains several educational robots (programmable devices that students can use to learn coding and automation) and robotics kits for building custom projects. An interactive digital whiteboard and high-performance computers with design software are available for collaborative work and multimedia projects. What makes the use of this technology special is the integration into the curriculum: technology is not just shown as a demo, but actively used by students to learn. For example, in art or geography class, students might use VR to virtually visit art museums or remote geographies, making lessons far more vivid. In chemistry, a 3D molecular modeling program plus 3D printing allows students to create models of molecules. The SmartLab also introduces technologies like a hydroponic station (as hinted by some SmartLab packages) to learn about smart agriculture, again blending tech with real-world applications. The innovative aspect is the holistic approach: multiple technologies are available in one space, encouraging interdisciplinary projects (a student designing an engineering prototype can 3D print it and program a robot to interact with it, all within the same lab). Overall, SmartLab Bucov exemplifies how modern hardware and software, from VR to 3D fabrication, can be harnessed to create an active learning playground that keeps students at the forefront of technological literacy.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Collaboration:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">The design and operation of SmartLab Bucov strongly promote teamwork and cooperation among students. The physical setup has cluster seating and open areas where students naturally gather to work together, rather than sit in isolated rows. Many learning activities in the lab are project-based and too complex for one student to do alone, which encourages students to form groups – for example, a team of students might divvy up roles to build a robotic car (one works on coding, another on design, another on testing). In using the VR equipment, students often work in pairs – one student navigates the virtual environment while another observes on a screen and they discuss the experience in real time. Such interactions build communication and team problem-solving skills. The creation of the lab itself was a collaborative educational experience: students worked side by side with architects to design the reading corner and the look of the space, giving them a sense of ownership and demonstrating a real-world collaboration between youth and professionals. This participatory design process taught students how to negotiate ideas and cooperate towards a common goal (a model that could be replicated for future student involvement in school improvements). Additionally, the SmartLab often hosts multi-grade workshops where older students mentor younger ones on using equipment like 3D printers, fostering a collaborative learning community across age groups. Teachers, too, collaborate in this space – it’s common for multiple teachers (e.g., an IT teacher and a science teacher) to co-run a SmartLab session that intersects their subjects, modeling collaboration in teaching. Finally, the SmartLab has become a hub for community collaboration: local businesses and the city council have visited and even held workshops in the lab, integrating community experts (like engineers or IT specialists) who collaborate with teachers to guide student projects. All these elements ensure that cooperation is intrinsic to the SmartLab experience, preparing students to work effectively in teams – a key tenet of active learning.</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th><strong>Feedback:</strong></th>
<th style="text-align: left;">The SmartLab environment provides numerous avenues for immediate and constructive feedback, essential for the active learning process. With many activities being hands-on and iterative, students receive instant feedback from the technology itself: a robot either works or doesn’t, providing prompt insight into whether the code was correct, or a 3D print may fail if a design flaw exists, signaling students to adjust their model. This trial-and-error learning, facilitated by technology, is guided by teachers who prompt students to reflect on why a failure occurred and how to fix it – a constructive feedback loop. The presence of interactive software also means students often get real-time on-screen feedback; for instance, coding platforms used in the lab can flag errors in real time, and VR educational programs can respond to student choices (like a virtual lab simulation giving immediate results for an experiment). Teachers in the SmartLab leverage these quick feedback mechanisms to have “teachable moments” on the spot. Because the teacher-to-student ratio during lab sessions is often lower (sometimes classes are split for lab work), instructors can circulate and give one-on-one feedback more readily than in a traditional classroom. They can quickly intervene if a group is stuck, ask probing questions, and provide hints without taking over the project, thus guiding students to the solution. Moreover, the SmartLab’s culture encourages peer feedback: students are commonly found reviewing each other’s designs or code, offering suggestions (for example, “Try making that gear bigger so it prints better”), which not only helps the recipient improve their work immediately but also reinforces the giver’s understanding. This open feedback culture is supported by the lab’s non-judgmental, experimental atmosphere – mistakes are seen as part of learning, and the quick iteration cycles mean students can rapidly implement feedback and see the results. In summary, SmartLab Bucov integrates continuous feedback through its technology (immediate outputs and responses) and its collaborative teaching approach, ensuring that learning is adjusted and improved in real time as students actively engage with the tasks.</th>
</tr>

<th colspan="2"><strong>2.6 Results</strong>:</th>
</tr>
<tr>
<th colspan="2" style="text-align: left;">Although relatively new, SmartLab Bucov and similar SmartLabs have begun to show promising results in the realm of TEAL. <strong>Qualitatively</strong>, student engagement is exceptionally high – teachers observe nearly 100% participation in lab activities, even from students who are usually disinterested in traditional classes. Students have developed tangible skills: in the first year, Bucov students created dozens of 3D printed models (from keychains to engineering prototypes), wrote their first lines of code for robots, and some even entered (and won prizes in) national coding and robotics competitions as a direct consequence of their SmartLab practice. The lab environment also improved soft skills; for instance, students became noticeably more confident in presenting their projects to peers and even to visiting officials, showing growth in communication abilities. <strong>Quantitatively</strong>, while standardized test improvements may take time to document, one immediate metric is participation and outreach: within a year, the SmartLab hosted over 10 community workshops and events, and an estimated 800+ people (students, teachers from other schools, parents, community members) had visited or utilized the lab in some capacity, spreading digital literacy beyond just the core student body. The success of Bucov’s lab has catalyzed replication – by 2023, at least <strong>20 SmartLabs</strong> (approximate figure) have been established or were in planning across different counties (some funded by local councils, others by EU funds), indicating that the model is being adopted as a best practice in educational innovation. Another outcome has been increased student retention and interest in science/tech careers: the school noted a decrease in absenteeism for classes held in the SmartLab, and surveys showed more students expressing interest in pursuing IT or engineering studies. In terms of teacher development, having the SmartLab has improved teacher morale and attracted new talent – educators are eager to teach in a school with such facilities, and existing staff gained new competencies through training on the lab’s equipment. The SmartLab’s results also align with community development: local companies have shown greater interest in partnering with the school (some offering internships or mentorship programs for students) after seeing the lab, thus creating a pipeline between education and industry in the area. In conclusion, the SmartLab Bucov illustrates how providing a technology-rich, active learning space in a rural school can yield benefits ranging from heightened student engagement and skill acquisition to serving as a replicable prototype that influences educational policy and investment on a broader scale.</th>

This collection of good practices is one of the key outcomes of the Erasmus+ project "**Drops of Sustainable Development**" and represents a valuable tool for adult educators and trainers. The project aims to provide innovative tools and strategies to integrate sustainable development skills into training programs, with a particular focus on the TEAL (Technology Enhanced Active Learning) methodology.

The practices presented in this document, selected through a rigorous process of research and analysis of educational and community projects, reflect the commitment of the **DROPS project** to promoting active, collaborative, and technology-enhanced learning. The selection criteria adopted (impact, innovation, replicability, participation, and sustainability) made it possible to identify initiatives that can effectively support educators in overcoming the difficulties of integrating sustainability into curricula, by offering practical and engaging methods.

The compilation of these practices has brought to light several important reflections, common to all the contributions. A central theme is the emphasis on student empowerment and the shift away from traditional teacher-centered models. Many projects actively involve students in the learning process, promoting critical thinking, problem-solving, and a sense of agency. This is evident in initiatives such as "Greener and Safer School Streets," where students become "local experts" in assessing and improving their own environment, and "**Eco-Scoala**" which establishes student-led Eco-Committees to promote sustainability efforts within schools.

This approach is closely linked to the contextualization of learning, another key theme that emerged from the practices, which highlight the importance of adapting educational interventions to the specific needs and characteristics of the territories, as illustrated by the "**WELL-BEING IN A MORE SUSTAINABLE NEIGHBOURHOOD**" and "**WARKA TOWER**" projects. This fosters meaningful learning and a greater impact of the actions undertaken, responding to the need, emphasized by the DROPS project, to provide practical and applicable tools.

Another key reflection is the integration of sustainability in various educational fields. The practices demonstrate that ESD is not limited to environmental science but can be effectively incorporated into different sectors such as adult education ("**5P Competences**"), vocational training ("**Apprenticeships for Greener Economies and Societies**"), and even the fashion industry ("**Rifò**"). This interdisciplinary approach highlights the interconnectedness of sustainability with all aspects of life and the importance of promoting a holistic understanding.

Furthermore, the document emphasizes the transformative power of technology in enhancing learning experiences. Projects such as "**CircuTex**" and "**ELEMENTS OF AI**" utilize virtual reality and online platforms to offer engaging and accessible educational opportunities. These initiatives demonstrate the potential of technology not only to provide content but also to create immersive environments, facilitate collaboration, and personalize learning.

Finally, the strong emphasis on collaboration and partnerships throughout the collection highlights the awareness that education for sustainable development is a shared responsibility. The success of many projects relies on the active involvement of various stakeholders, including schools, universities, local communities, NGOs, and industry partners. This spirit of collaboration promotes knowledge sharing, resource mobilization, and the creation of support ecosystems for sustainable development.

In conclusion, this collection of good practices represents a valuable resource for the **DROPS project**, providing concrete and stimulating examples of how ESD can be effectively integrated into adult education through innovative methodologies such as TEAL. It is hoped that these examples can inspire educators and trainers to develop transformative educational paths, capable of forming aware and active citizens in the construction of a more sustainable future.

**Recommendations for Adaptation**

The diverse range of practices presented offers numerous opportunities for adaptation and synergy. To effectively adapt these approaches to the project's target group, the following recommendations can be considered:

- **Contextualization**: Adapting the practices to the specific local context is essential. This implies considering the unique environmental, social, and economic challenges and opportunities that the target group faces.

- **Integration**: Combining elements from different practices can create more complete and impactful interventions. For example, integrating the community engagement strategies of "Well-being in a More Sustainable Neighbourhood" with the technological tools of "**InnoVET**" could promote both local action and digital literacy.

- **Scalability**: Prioritizing practices that can be easily scaled and replicated is essential for a broader impact. The "Patrula de Reciclare" program, with its adaptable "recycling patrol" model, provides a good example of a practice that can be implemented in various contexts.

These possible combinations and adaptations will be the subject of exploration and experimentation during the LTTA in Spain, which will involve 12 adult educators from the 5 partner countries. Their experiences and feedback will also contribute to defining the project’s training materials, in particular the “**DSD_Toolkit**”, which will include teaching modules for educators and facilitators on ESD, based on practical, engaging, and action-oriented methods, using the TEAL methodology.

In conclusion, this collection of good practices represents a valuable resource for the DROPS project, providing concrete and stimulating examples of how ESD can be effectively integrated into adult education through innovative methodologies such as TEAL. It is hoped that these examples can inspire educators and trainers to develop transformative educational paths, capable of forming aware and active citizens in the construction of a more sustainable future.
